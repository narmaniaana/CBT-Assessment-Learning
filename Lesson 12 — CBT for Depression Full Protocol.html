CBT for Depression

Session-by-Session Full Protocol Guide — From Assessment to Relapse Prevention
📖 ~30 min read 🎓 7 sections + exam 📄 12-20 session protocol

🌱 1. Overview of CBT for Depression

Beck's Cognitive Model

Aaron Beck's cognitive model of depression rests on a central insight: depression is maintained not merely by sad mood, but by systematic distortions in how the person thinks about three domains — the negative cognitive triad:

The Negative Cognitive Triad

  • Self: "I am worthless / defective / a failure"
  • World: "Nothing ever works out / people don't care / the world is unfair"
  • Future: "Things will never get better / it's hopeless / why bother trying"

These beliefs operate at multiple levels:

  • Core beliefs (deep, absolute schemas formed early in life)
  • Intermediate beliefs (rules, attitudes, assumptions — e.g., "If I'm not perfect, I'm a failure")
  • Automatic thoughts (quick, situation-specific — e.g., "I'm going to mess this up")

Evidence Base

Severity Best Evidence
Mild depression CBT alone is highly effective (comparable to medication)
Moderate depression CBT alone or combined with medication; both work well
Severe depression Combined CBT + medication is superior to either alone
Recurrent depression CBT has better relapse prevention than medication alone (approx. 50% lower relapse rates)

Key Treatment Targets

  1. Behavioral withdrawal — reduced activity, loss of pleasure, social isolation
  2. Negative automatic thoughts — distorted interpretations of events
  3. Intermediate beliefs — rigid rules and dysfunctional assumptions
  4. Core beliefs — schemas of worthlessness, helplessness, unlovability

Typical Course

Standard protocol: 12-20 sessions, typically weekly, with the final sessions spaced out. Treatment progresses from behavioral strategies (early) to deeper cognitive work (middle-late), ending with relapse prevention.

📋 2. Phase 1: Assessment & Engagement Sessions 1-3

Session 1 — Building the Foundation

Goals: Build rapport, assess symptoms, provide psychoeducation, socialize to the CBT model, set initial goals.

Key Assessments:

  • PHQ-9 — Quick 9-item self-report; scores: 5 mild, 10 moderate, 15 moderately severe, 20+ severe
  • BDI-II — More detailed 21-item measure; useful for tracking week-to-week change
  • Item 9 on either scale screens for suicidal ideation — always review this item with the client

Socializing to the CBT Model: Use the client's own example. Pick a recent situation where they felt low and walk through it:

Session 1 Script — Socializing to the CBT Model

Therapist: "You mentioned that on Monday morning you felt really low. Can you tell me more about what was happening?"

Client: "I woke up and just thought, what's the point of getting up. I stayed in bed until noon."

Therapist: "So the thought was 'what's the point of getting up.' And when you had that thought, what did you feel emotionally?"

Client: "Hopeless. Tired. Just... heavy."

Therapist: "And what did you do?"

Client: "Stayed in bed. Didn't shower. Cancelled meeting with a friend."

Therapist: "So here we can see how it all connects — the thought 'what's the point' led to feeling hopeless, which led to staying in bed and cancelling plans. And what happened to your mood after staying in bed all morning?"

Client: "It got worse, actually."

Therapist: "Exactly. This is a really important observation. The thing we did to cope — staying in bed — actually made the depression stronger. This is what we call a vicious cycle, and it's exactly what we're going to work on breaking together."

Session 2 — Activity Monitoring

Goals: Begin activity monitoring, introduce the mood-activity connection, start behavioral activation.

  • Introduce an activity log — hourly or by time blocks, tracking what they did + mood rating (0-10)
  • Explain the rationale: "We want to discover which activities lift your mood and which drag it down"
  • Often clients are surprised to discover patterns they hadn't noticed
Session 2 Script — Introducing Activity Monitoring

Therapist: "This week I'd like us to try something. It's a simple log — you just note what you're doing at different times of day, and rate your mood from 0 to 10. You don't need to change anything — just notice."

Client: "I feel like my mood is just bad all the time though."

Therapist: "That's what depression tells us — that it's all the same flat gray. But usually when people track carefully, they find small differences. Maybe a 2 at one point and a 4 at another. Those differences are really important — they show us where we can start building."

Session 3 — Activation Begins

Goals: Review activity monitoring, begin activity scheduling, start building case conceptualization.

  • Review the activity log together — identify patterns: which activities = higher mood? lower mood?
  • Introduce mastery (M) and pleasure (P) ratings alongside mood
  • Mastery activities: things that give a sense of accomplishment (even small — taking a shower, making a meal)
  • Pleasure activities: things that bring even small enjoyment
  • Schedule 2-3 activities for the coming week — start very small

Clinical Tip: For severely depressed clients, "take a shower" or "walk to the mailbox" can be a meaningful mastery activity. Meet the client where they are, not where you think they should be.

Session 3 Script — Beginning Activity Scheduling

Therapist: "Looking at your log, I notice that on Wednesday when you went for a short walk, your mood was a 4 — that was actually the highest rating all week. What do you make of that?"

Client: "I guess... I don't know, it was only 4."

Therapist: "When most of the week is 1s and 2s, a 4 is actually significant. It tells us something. What if we planned a couple of short walks this week — not because you 'should,' but as an experiment to see if it helps?"

Client: "I can try... but what if I don't feel like it?"

Therapist: "That's exactly what depression does — it takes away the motivation first. So we use a different approach: we act first and let the motivation follow. You don't wait to feel like doing it. You schedule it and do it, and then see how you feel after."

🛠 3. Phase 2: Skill Building Sessions 4-8

Introducing Thought Records

The thought record is the workhorse of CBT for depression. Start simple and build up:

Stage Columns Purpose
First (Sessions 4-5) 3-column: Situation → Thought → Emotion Learn to catch automatic thoughts
Then (Sessions 5-6) 5-column: Add Evidence For + Evidence Against Begin evaluating thoughts
Full (Sessions 6-8) 7-column: Add Balanced Thought + Re-rate Emotion Full cognitive restructuring

Identifying Cognitive Distortions

Teach clients to recognize patterns in their thinking:

  • All-or-nothing thinking: "If I can't do it perfectly, I'm a total failure"
  • Mental filter: Focusing only on the one negative comment, ignoring ten positives
  • Fortune telling: "This will definitely go badly"
  • Mind reading: "They think I'm boring"
  • Overgeneralization: "This always happens to me"
  • Disqualifying the positive: "That doesn't count because..."
  • Emotional reasoning: "I feel like a failure, so I must be one"

Framing Tip: Never tell a client their thinking is "wrong" or "distorted." Instead: "Let's look at this thought and see how accurate it really is — like a scientist examining the evidence."

Socratic Questioning

Key questions for challenging negative automatic thoughts:

  1. "What is the evidence that this thought is true? What is the evidence against it?"
  2. "Is there an alternative explanation?"
  3. "What would you say to a friend who had this thought?"
  4. "What's the worst that could happen? The best? The most realistic?"
  5. "What's the effect of believing this thought? What would be the effect of changing it?"

Behavioral Experiments

Move beyond just talking about thoughts — test them in real life:

Example: Testing the thought "Nobody wants to hear from me"

Prediction: "If I text my old friend, they will ignore me or respond coldly."

Experiment: Text one friend this week and observe what happens.

Outcome: Friend replied within an hour, seemed happy to hear from them, suggested meeting for coffee.

Learning: My prediction was based on depression, not reality. Some people actually do want to hear from me.

Identifying Intermediate Beliefs

As therapy progresses, begin noticing the rules and assumptions underneath the automatic thoughts:

  • Rules: "I must always put others first"
  • Attitudes: "Asking for help is a sign of weakness"
  • Assumptions: "If I show my true self, people will reject me"

💡 4. Phase 3: Core Belief Work Sessions 9-14

The Downward Arrow Technique

Used to uncover core beliefs by following the meaning of automatic thoughts down to their root:

Therapist: "You said 'I failed that test.' And if that were true — what would that mean about you?"

Client: "That I'm not smart enough."

Therapist: "And if you're not smart enough — what does that mean?"

Client: "That I'll never succeed at anything."

Therapist: "And if you never succeed at anything — what does that mean about you as a person?"

Client: "That I'm... worthless."

Therapist: "So we've arrived at a core belief: 'I am worthless.' This is the deep belief that all those surface thoughts connect to. This is what we need to work on."

Core Belief Log

Once a core belief is identified, the client tracks evidence over multiple situations across days and weeks:

Situation Evidence "I am worthless" Evidence against "I am worthless"
Coworker asked me for help — They value my opinion enough to ask
Made a mistake at work Felt incompetent (but is a feeling evidence?) Boss said it was minor and easily fixed
Friend cancelled dinner Thought "they don't want to see me" They rescheduled for next week — they wanted to see me

The Continuum Technique

Core beliefs are often all-or-nothing. The continuum helps introduce shades of gray:

Instead of "worthless" vs "valuable," create a 0-100 scale. Ask the client to place themselves and others on the continuum. This breaks the dichotomous thinking.

Key question: "On a scale of 0-100, where 0 is completely worthless and 100 is the most valuable person alive — where would you put yourself? Where would you put [someone they respect]? What makes that person a 70 and not a 100?"

Historical Review

Trace the origins of the core belief through developmental history:

  • When did you first start believing "I am worthless"?
  • What experiences seemed to confirm this belief?
  • Were there experiences that contradicted it — that you perhaps discounted?
  • Could there be an alternative explanation for those early experiences?

Developing Alternative Core Beliefs

Don't just weaken the old belief — build a new one:

  • Old belief: "I am worthless"
  • New belief: "I have value, even though I'm not perfect"
  • Track evidence for the new belief daily
  • Use behavioral experiments to generate new evidence

Behavioral Experiments Targeting Core Beliefs

Example: Testing "I am unlovable"

Old belief: "I am fundamentally unlovable. If people really knew me, they'd leave."

Experiment: Share one genuine vulnerability with a trusted friend.

Prediction: "They'll pull away or be uncomfortable."

Outcome: Friend thanked them for being honest, shared their own struggle, relationship deepened.

New learning: "When I'm genuine, some people actually come closer — not further away."

Creating the Relapse Prevention Plan

  1. Identify early warning signs — What are the first signs that depression might be returning?
    • Sleep changes (sleeping more, waking early)
    • Social withdrawal (cancelling plans, not returning calls)
    • Return of old automatic thoughts ("What's the point?")
    • Reduced activity or pleasure
  2. Create an action plan — What to do at first sign of warning:
    • Resume activity scheduling immediately
    • Fill out a thought record
    • Contact a support person
    • Call the therapist for a booster session if needed

The Therapy Blueprint

A document the client creates summarizing their entire therapy journey:

Blueprint Sections:

  1. What I came in with: My depression, main symptoms, PHQ-9 score
  2. What I learned about how depression works: The cognitive model, my vicious cycles
  3. My core beliefs and how we changed them: Old belief → New belief
  4. Skills that work for me: Activity scheduling, thought records, behavioral experiments
  5. My early warning signs: List specific personal indicators
  6. My action plan: Steps to take if depression starts returning
  7. People I can reach out to: Names and contacts

Spacing Out Sessions

Phase Frequency Purpose
Sessions 1-14 Weekly Active treatment
Sessions 15-17 Every 2 weeks Testing independence
Sessions 18-20 Monthly Consolidation
Boosters As needed (3, 6, 12 months) Maintenance

⚠️ 6. Common Challenges in Treating Depression

Client Too Depressed to Do Homework

Solution: Simplify radically. If a thought record is too much, start with: "Just text me one word for your mood each evening." Any tiny action breaks the inertia. Homework should be at the level where success is almost guaranteed.

Chronic / Recurrent Depression

  • Standard CBT may need extension beyond 20 sessions
  • Consider Schema Therapy for longstanding patterns rooted in early experiences
  • Consider MBCT (Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy) for relapse prevention in recurrent depression (3+ episodes)
  • Medication consultation should be considered if not already in place

Suicidal Ideation

This is not a reason to stop CBT. It is a reason to add specific interventions:

  • Safety planning (Stanley & Brown model): Warning signs → Coping strategies → People to contact → Professionals to call → Making the environment safe
  • Increase session frequency temporarily
  • Address hopelessness directly — it's the strongest predictor of suicide, more than depression severity
  • Use problem-solving for any real-world crises
  • Coordinate with prescriber for medication adjustment

Comorbid Anxiety

  • Depression + anxiety is the rule, not the exception (up to 60% comorbidity)
  • Treat simultaneously with an integrated approach
  • Behavioral activation helps both (activity reduces rumination and worry)
  • Cognitive restructuring applies to both depressive and anxious thoughts
  • If avoidance is prominent, add exposure components
Decision Guide: When standard CBT for depression isn't enough
Situation Consider Adding
No improvement by Session 6 Review case conceptualization, consider medication, check therapeutic alliance
Deep early-life schemas Schema Therapy techniques (limited reparenting, imagery rescripting)
3+ episodes of recurrence MBCT after acute treatment
Persistent rumination Rumination-focused CBT, metacognitive therapy
Interpersonal problems maintaining depression Interpersonal techniques, CBASP for chronic depression

👤 7. Case Example: Nino's Full Treatment Arc

Nino, 34, software engineer. Referred by GP after 4 months of low mood, poor sleep, loss of interest. PHQ-9 = 18 (moderately severe). No prior therapy. Currently on no medication.

Presenting complaints: Fatigue, social withdrawal, difficulty concentrating at work, feelings of worthlessness, insomnia (early morning awakening).

Session 1 — Assessment & Engagement

Built rapport. Administered PHQ-9 (score = 18). Socialized to CBT model using Nino's example of avoiding a team meeting ("I thought I had nothing useful to say → felt worthless → skipped the meeting → felt guilty"). Set goal: "Be able to function at work and enjoy time with friends again." Assigned activity monitoring.

Session 4 — First Thought Record

PHQ-9 = 15. Behavioral activation showing small gains. Introduced 3-column thought record. Key automatic thought identified: "If I speak up at work, people will realize I'm incompetent."

Nino's First 3-Column Thought Record
Situation Automatic Thought Emotion
Team meeting, boss asked for opinions "I have nothing smart to say. They'll think I'm incompetent." Anxiety 8/10, Shame 7/10
Friend texted asking to hang out "I'll just bring the mood down. They're being polite." Sadness 7/10, Guilt 6/10
Saturday morning, nothing planned "What's the point of doing anything?" Hopelessness 8/10, Emptiness 7/10

Session 8 — Behavioral Experiment Success

PHQ-9 = 12. Major breakthrough: Nino tested the prediction "If I share an idea at the meeting, people will think it's stupid." He spoke up — his manager said "great point" and added it to the project plan. This was a powerful disconfirmation of his negative prediction.

Session 12 — Core Belief Work

PHQ-9 = 8. Using downward arrow, identified core belief: "I am not good enough." Began core belief log. Historical review revealed this belief originated from a very critical father who rarely expressed approval. Started developing alternative belief: "I am competent and capable, even when I make mistakes."

Session 16 — Relapse Prevention

PHQ-9 = 5 (mild, subclinical). Created therapy blueprint together. Nino identified his early warning signs: skipping the gym, declining social invitations, and the return of "what's the point" thoughts. Created action plan. Spaced sessions to monthly.

Outcome: Nino completed 18 sessions over 6 months. PHQ-9 dropped from 18 to 4 at final session. At 6-month follow-up booster: PHQ-9 = 3. He reported using thought records independently about once a week and had maintained his activity schedule. No relapse.

📝 Final Scored Exam

8 multiple-choice questions covering the CBT for Depression protocol. Score: 7-8 Excellent, 5-6 Good, 0-4 Needs Review.

1. What are the three components of Beck's negative cognitive triad in depression?

Correct! The negative cognitive triad = negative views of the self ("I am worthless"), the world ("nothing works out"), and the future ("things will never improve").
The answer is B. The negative cognitive triad = negative views of the self, the world, and the future — not the general CBT model components (thoughts, feelings, behaviors).

2. In the first session for depression, which of the following should you NOT typically do?

Correct! The full 7-column thought record is introduced gradually (Sessions 6-8). Session 1 focuses on assessment, psychoeducation, rapport, and goal-setting.
The answer is C. The 7-column thought record is far too advanced for Session 1. It's introduced gradually over Sessions 4-8. Session 1 is about assessment, rapport, and socialization to the model.

3. What does the behavioral activation principle "act first, let motivation follow" mean?

Correct! Depression removes motivation, so the standard sequence (motivation → action) doesn't work. Behavioral activation reverses it: action → motivation/mood improvement.
The answer is B. Depression breaks the normal motivation → action sequence. Behavioral activation reverses it: schedule the activity, do it, and then often the mood and motivation improve afterward.

4. What is the purpose of the downward arrow technique?

Correct! The downward arrow follows the chain of meaning from an automatic thought down to the underlying core belief ("If that's true, what does it mean about you?").
The answer is B. The downward arrow technique traces meaning from surface automatic thoughts down to core beliefs by repeatedly asking "And if that were true, what would that mean about you?"

5. A client with moderate depression isn't doing any homework because they feel "too tired." What is the best initial response?

Correct! The key principle: simplify until success is almost guaranteed. Any small action breaks inertia and can be built upon. "Try harder" is the opposite of therapeutic.
The answer is C. When clients are too depressed for standard homework, simplify radically. Even a tiny task (texting one mood word) creates momentum. Telling them to "try harder" reinforces their belief that they're failing.

6. For severe depression, what does the evidence suggest is the most effective approach?

Correct! For severe depression, the combined approach (CBT + medication) is superior to either treatment alone.
The answer is C. Research consistently shows that for severe depression, combined treatment (CBT + medication) outperforms either modality alone.

7. When creating a therapy blueprint for relapse prevention, which element is MOST important?

Correct! The most critical element is personalized: the client's own early warning signs paired with a specific action plan. This makes relapse prevention practical and actionable.
The answer is B. A therapy blueprint must be personalized and actionable. The client's specific early warning signs + their concrete action plan are what actually prevent relapse. Generic lists of distortions or session summaries are far less useful.

8. At what point in treatment should you begin spacing out sessions (from weekly to biweekly)?

Correct! Sessions are spaced during Phase 4 (Consolidation & Relapse Prevention), typically around Sessions 15+, as a way to test the client's ability to maintain gains independently.
The answer is C. Spacing begins during the consolidation phase (around Session 15+), after core belief work is done and the client is clearly improving. It serves as a test of independence before termination.
