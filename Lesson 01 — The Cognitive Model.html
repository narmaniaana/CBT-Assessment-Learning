1. What is CBT?

A brief history and core premise

CBT stands for Cognitive Behavioral Therapy. It is one of the most widely researched and evidence-based forms of psychotherapy in the world.

🕐 A Brief History

In the 1960s, psychiatrist Aaron T. Beck noticed that his depressed patients had a steady stream of negative thoughts running through their minds — thoughts they often didn't even notice. He called these automatic thoughts. Rather than focusing only on unconscious conflicts (as psychoanalysis did), Beck developed a therapy that directly targets these thoughts and their impact on mood and behavior.

CBT was originally developed for depression, but has since been adapted for anxiety, PTSD, OCD, eating disorders, chronic pain, insomnia, and many more conditions.

💡 The Core Premise of CBT
It is not events themselves that upset us — it is our interpretation of those events. Two people can face the exact same situation and feel completely different emotions, because they think about it differently.
"It's not things that disturb us, but our judgments about things."
— Epictetus, Stoic philosopher (1st century AD)

Beck recognized that this ancient Stoic idea is the bedrock of cognitive therapy. The philosophical roots go back 2,000 years, but Beck gave us a systematic, scientifically testable way to apply it in the therapy room.

📈 How CBT Differs from Other Approaches

Dimension 🧠 CBT 🛌 Psychodynamic 🌿 Humanistic ⚖️ DBT
Primary Focus Present thoughts & behaviors Past experiences & unconscious Self-actualization & growth Emotion regulation & distress tolerance
Typical Duration 12–20 sessions Months to years Open-ended ~1 year (standard program)
Structure Highly structured, agenda-driven Unstructured, free-flowing Client-led Structured skills groups + individual
Core Tool Thought records Free association Empathic reflection Diary cards
Homework Yes — central to therapy Rarely Sometimes Yes — skills practice
🤔 Self-check: Why is CBT considered "time-limited"?

Because CBT is goal-directed and skill-based. Once a client learns to identify and challenge their distorted thoughts, they become their own therapist. The typical course is 12–20 sessions, making it more time-efficient than many other approaches.

2. The ABC Model

Ellis's model and Beck's cognitive theory

Albert Ellis (a contemporary of Beck) created a simple but powerful framework called the ABC Model. It captures the core insight of cognitive therapy in three letters:

A
Activating Event
The situation or trigger — something that happens in the world
B
Beliefs
Your thoughts, interpretations, and meaning-making about the event
C
Consequences
Emotional, behavioral, and physical responses that follow
🔑 The Key Insight
Most people believe: A → C (event causes emotion).
The truth is: A → B → C (event triggers beliefs, which cause emotions).

Same event + different thoughts = different emotions.

📧 Example: The Boss's Email

Three employees receive the same email from their boss: "See me in my office tomorrow morning."

😨

Person 1: Anxious

Thought: "I'm getting fired. I knew this was coming."

Emotion: Anxiety, dread

Behavior: Can't sleep, rehearses excuses

😁

Person 2: Excited

Thought: "Maybe I'm getting a promotion!"

Emotion: Excitement, hope

Behavior: Tells partner the good news

😐

Person 3: Neutral

Thought: "Probably about the project update."

Emotion: Neutral, mild curiosity

Behavior: Prepares project notes, goes to bed normally

✅ What does this prove?
The same event (A) produced three completely different emotional outcomes (C). The only variable that changed was B — the belief. This is the foundation of all cognitive therapy: if you change the thought, you change the feeling.
🤔 Self-check: In the ABC model, what does "B" stand for and why is it the most important letter?

B = Beliefs (thoughts, interpretations, meanings). It is the most important because it is the mediating variable — the filter between what happens and how we feel. CBT works by targeting B, because that is what we can actually change. We often cannot control A (events), but we can learn to examine and modify B.

3. The Five-Part Model (Hot Cross Bun)

The interconnected CBT diagram

Beck expanded the simple ABC model into a richer, more nuanced picture. The five-part model (sometimes called the "Hot Cross Bun" because of its crisscross shape) shows how five elements interact:

🌎Situation
💭Thoughts
❤️Emotions
🦷Physical
🏃Behaviors

All five elements are interconnected — each one influences all the others.

🔎 Worked Example: Laughter in the Room

A client walks into a room and hears people laughing. Here are two completely different ways the model can play out:

🔴 Interpretation A

  • Situation: Walks into room, hears laughter
  • Thought: "They're laughing at me"
  • Emotion: Shame, anxiety
  • Physical: Face flushes, stomach tightens
  • Behavior: Leaves the room, avoids future social gatherings

🟢 Interpretation B

  • Situation: Walks into room, hears laughter
  • Thought: "Someone told a funny joke"
  • Emotion: Neutral, curious
  • Physical: Relaxed body
  • Behavior: Joins the group, asks what's funny
💡 Therapist Takeaway
When formulating a case, always map the client's experience across all five domains. This gives you and the client a complete picture — and multiple entry points for intervention. You can change thoughts, modify behaviors, address physical symptoms, or work with emotions directly.
🤔 Self-check: Name all five elements of the Hot Cross Bun model

1. Situation (trigger)   2. Thoughts (cognitions)   3. Emotions (feelings)   4. Physical sensations (body)   5. Behaviors (actions)

All five are interconnected and each influences the others.

4. Levels of Cognition

From surface thoughts to deep beliefs

Not all thoughts are created equal. Beck identified three levels of cognition, each progressively deeper and harder to access:

🌊 Awareness Line
⚡ Automatic Thoughts
Quick, reflexive, situation-specific. Often go unnoticed.
"She ignored me."   "I'm going to fail this."
📜 Intermediate Beliefs
Rules, assumptions, and attitudes. Conditional in nature.
"If people ignore me, it means they don't like me."
"I must always perform perfectly or I'm a failure."
💧 Core Beliefs
Deep, global, unconditional. Formed in childhood. Hardest to change.
"I am unlovable."   "The world is dangerous."   "I am incompetent."

🧬 Understanding the Three Levels

⚡ Level 1: Automatic Thoughts
These are the thoughts that pop into our heads spontaneously. They are situation-specific and often take the form of brief phrases or images. Clients usually become aware of these first. Examples: "She thinks I'm stupid," "This will go wrong," "I can't handle this."
📜 Level 2: Intermediate Beliefs
These are the rules and assumptions that generate automatic thoughts. They often follow "If... then..." patterns or take the form of "should" statements. Examples: "If I make a mistake, people will reject me," "I should always be in control."
💧 Level 3: Core Beliefs
The deepest level. These are absolute, global, unconditional beliefs about self, others, and the world. They are like the operating system of the mind — running in the background, shaping everything. Usually formed during childhood. Examples: "I am worthless," "People can't be trusted," "I am defective."

In early CBT sessions, therapists focus on automatic thoughts (the tip of the iceberg). As therapy progresses, you work your way down toward intermediate and eventually core beliefs.

🤔 Self-check: A client says "If I don't get an A, I'm a total failure." What level of cognition is this?

This is an intermediate belief (rule/assumption). It follows the "If... then..." conditional pattern. The underlying core belief might be: "I am incompetent" (unconditional, global).

5. Why This Matters for Therapy

From theory to clinical application

Understanding the cognitive model isn't just academic — it is the engine of everything you will do as a CBT therapist.

⚡ The Mechanism of Change in CBT
If you change what someone thinks → you change how they feel and what they do.

CBT targets thoughts (at all three levels) as the primary lever for therapeutic change.

⚠️ Important Distinction: NOT "Positive Thinking"

CBT is not about replacing negative thoughts with positive ones. It is not about saying "just think happy thoughts!" That would be naive and invalidating.

❌ NOT the goal

Distorted thought: "I'll definitely fail."

Toxic positivity: "I'll definitely succeed! Everything is great!"

✅ The actual goal

Distorted thought: "I'll definitely fail."

Balanced thought: "I might struggle, but I've prepared well. Let me see how it goes."

The goal of CBT is to move from distorted thinking to balanced, evidence-based thinking. We help clients evaluate their thoughts like a scientist — examining the evidence for and against, considering alternative explanations, and arriving at a more realistic conclusion.

🤔 Self-check: A colleague says "CBT is just about thinking positively." How would you correct them?

CBT is about realistic, evidence-based thinking, not positive thinking. We don't replace negative thoughts with unrealistically positive ones. Instead, we help clients examine whether their thoughts are accurate, consider alternative perspectives, and develop more balanced conclusions. Sometimes a negative thought is accurate — in that case, we help the client problem-solve rather than change the thought.

6. Full Clinical Walkthrough: Dato's Case

Applying the model from assessment to intervention
📋 Client Profile
Name: Dato, age 30
Presenting problem: Intense anxiety after a job interview
Context: The interviewer did not smile during the interview

Step 1: Map Using the Five-Part Model

🌎
Situation: The interviewer maintained a neutral, unsmiling expression throughout the interview.
💭
Automatic Thought: "I blew it. They hated me. I'm never going to get this job."
❤️
Emotions: Anxiety (85/100), Sadness (60/100)
🦷
Physical Sensations: Racing heart, sweaty palms, tight chest
🏃
Behavior: Checks phone obsessively for a response. Frantically applies to 10 more jobs that night. Difficulty sleeping.

Step 2: Challenge the Thought

As a therapist, you would help Dato examine his automatic thought. Ask questions like:

  • "What evidence do you have that you 'blew it'?"
  • "Is there another explanation for the interviewer's neutral face?"
  • "Have you ever had an interview go well where the interviewer didn't smile much?"

Step 3: See How a New Thought Changes Everything

🔴 Original Pattern

  • Thought: "I blew it, they hated me"
  • Emotion: Anxiety 85%, Sadness 60%
  • Physical: Racing heart, sweaty palms
  • Behavior: Obsessive phone checking, frantic applying

🟢 After Cognitive Restructuring

  • Thought: "Interviewers often maintain a neutral face; it doesn't mean I did poorly"
  • Emotion: Mild anxiety 30%, Hope 50%
  • Physical: Calmer breathing, relaxed shoulders
  • Behavior: Waits patiently, prepares calmly for other opportunities
💡 Notice: The situation didn't change. The interviewer's face was the same. But by changing the thought, everything downstream shifted — the emotions became less intense, the body calmed down, and the behavior became more adaptive. This is the power of the cognitive model.
🤔 Self-check: What deeper beliefs might be driving Dato's automatic thoughts?

Intermediate belief: "If an interviewer doesn't smile, it means they don't like me and I won't get the job."

Core belief: "I am not good enough" or "I am inadequate."

These deeper beliefs explain why Dato jumps to the worst-case interpretation. In later sessions, a CBT therapist would explore and gradually modify these beliefs.

Practice Exercises

Test your understanding before the exam

Exercise 1: Identify the ABC

A student gets a B+ on an exam and feels devastated. Break this down:

🔒 Reveal Answer

A (Activating Event): Received a B+ on the exam

B (Belief): "A B+ is terrible. I should have gotten an A. I'm falling behind."

C (Consequence): Devastation, sadness, possibly shame. May study obsessively or give up.

Note: A B+ is objectively a good grade. The emotional devastation comes from the belief/interpretation, not the event itself.

Exercise 2: Map the Five-Part Model

A client's partner doesn't reply to a text for 3 hours. The client becomes extremely anxious. Fill in all five parts:

🔒 Reveal Answer

Situation: Partner hasn't replied to text for 3 hours

Thought: "They're ignoring me. They're probably angry. Maybe they want to break up."

Emotion: Anxiety, fear, insecurity

Physical: Tight chest, restlessness, nausea

Behavior: Sends multiple follow-up texts, checks social media, calls partner repeatedly

Alternative thought: "They might be busy at work or their phone died. Three hours isn't unusual."

Exercise 3: Identify the Level of Cognition

Classify each statement:

  1. "My boss looked annoyed in the meeting" — what level?
  2. "If my boss is annoyed, I'll get fired" — what level?
  3. "I am fundamentally incompetent" — what level?
🔒 Reveal Answer
  1. Automatic thought — situation-specific, quick observation/interpretation
  2. Intermediate belief — conditional rule (If... then...)
  3. Core belief — global, unconditional, absolute statement about self

Exercise 4: Positive Thinking vs. Balanced Thinking

A client says: "I'm going to fail the exam." Write both an unrealistically positive response and a CBT-style balanced response.

🔒 Reveal Answer

Toxic positivity: "No way! You're going to ace it! You're amazing!"

Balanced (CBT) response: "I feel underprepared, but I've studied for 20 hours and passed practice tests. I may not get a perfect score, but there's good evidence I can pass. Let me focus on the areas I'm less confident in."

The balanced version acknowledges the anxiety while also examining the evidence.
