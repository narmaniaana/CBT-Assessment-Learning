CBT for Anxiety Disorders

Disorder-Specific Protocols & Treatment — GAD, Panic, Social Anxiety, and Phobias
📖 ~30 min read 🎓 6 sections + exam 📄 4 disorder protocols

🔬 1. The CBT Model of Anxiety

Core Mechanism

All anxiety disorders share a common cognitive foundation — a double distortion:

Overestimation of threat — "Something bad WILL happen" (probability is inflated)

Underestimation of coping — "And I WON'T be able to handle it" (resources are minimized)

Anxiety = Perceived Threat (too high) ÷ Perceived Coping (too low)

The Maintenance Cycle

Anxiety is maintained — not caused — by avoidance. Here is the core vicious cycle:

Perceived Threat
"Something bad will happen"
➜
Anxiety
Physical + cognitive symptoms
➜
Avoidance / Safety Behavior
"I need to escape / protect myself"
➜
Short-term Relief
Anxiety drops = behavior is reinforced
➜
No Disconfirmation
"I never learned it was safe"

The key insight: avoidance prevents the person from ever learning that the feared outcome doesn't happen (or that they could cope if it did). This is why exposure works — it provides the disconfirmation that avoidance blocks.

The Role of Safety Behaviors

Safety behaviors are subtle forms of avoidance that people use while still technically "facing" the feared situation:

  • Carrying medication "just in case" (even if never taking it)
  • Always sitting near the exit
  • Avoiding eye contact during conversation
  • Over-preparing for presentations
  • Checking body for symptoms

Why safety behaviors matter: A person can go to 100 parties and still believe "parties are dangerous" if they use safety behaviors each time. They attribute their survival to the safety behavior ("I was okay because I stayed near the door"), not to the situation actually being safe. Dropping safety behaviors is essential for real learning.

👨‍💻 2. Generalized Anxiety Disorder GAD

Dugas' Intolerance of Uncertainty Model

GAD is fundamentally about intolerance of uncertainty — the belief that uncertainty itself is threatening, unbearable, and must be resolved. People with GAD worry not because they face more problems, but because they cannot tolerate not knowing.

Core belief in GAD: "If I don't know for sure that something bad won't happen, I have to keep worrying about it."

Worry functions as an (unsuccessful) attempt to gain certainty and control.

Treatment Components

1. Worry Awareness Training

Many GAD clients don't even realize when they're worrying — it's so automatic. First step: learn to catch it.

  • Set random alarms throughout the day: "Am I worrying right now? About what?"
  • Keep a worry log: situation, worry content, anxiety level (0-10)
  • Notice the physical signals that accompany worry (tension, restlessness, shallow breathing)

2. Productive vs. Unproductive Worry

Productive Worry Unproductive Worry
About a real, current problem About a hypothetical "what if" scenario
You can take action now No action is currently possible
Leads to a plan → you can stop worrying Goes in circles — never resolves
Example: "I need to pay rent by Friday" Example: "What if I lose my job someday?"

3. Worry Postponement

Set a daily "worry time" — a specific 20-minute window:

Therapist: "When a worry pops up outside of worry time, you acknowledge it — 'I notice I'm worrying about X' — write it down, and then say to yourself: 'I'll think about this at 6 PM during worry time.' Then redirect your attention to what you're doing."

Client: "But what if I forget about it?"

Therapist: "You've written it down, so it's captured. And honestly — if you forget about it, that tells you something important about how urgent it actually was."

4. Problem-Solving Training (for Productive Worries)

  • Define the problem specifically
  • Brainstorm solutions (without judging)
  • Evaluate pros/cons of each
  • Choose one and make an action plan
  • Implement and review

5. Imaginal Exposure (for Unproductive Worries)

For "what if" worries that can't be solved, use imaginal exposure: write out the worst-case scenario in detail, read it repeatedly until anxiety habituates, then ask: "Could I cope with this? What would I actually do?"

6. Behavioral Experiments Targeting Uncertainty Tolerance

Example: Testing intolerance of uncertainty

Belief: "If I don't check everything twice, something bad will happen."

Experiment: Send an email without re-reading it three times. Just check once and send.

Prediction: "There will be an embarrassing typo and my colleague will think I'm careless."

Outcome: Colleague replied normally. No comment about errors. (There was actually one small typo — nobody noticed or cared.)

Learning: The world tolerates imperfection far more than I thought. Uncertainty about small things is actually fine.

Session-by-Session Outline (12-16 Sessions)

GAD Treatment Session Outline
Sessions 1-2 Assessment (GAD-7), psychoeducation about worry and intolerance of uncertainty, begin worry awareness training
Sessions 3-4 Distinguish productive vs unproductive worry, introduce worry postponement, begin problem-solving for real problems
Sessions 5-7 Cognitive restructuring of worry beliefs, behavioral experiments targeting uncertainty, imaginal exposure for worst-case scenarios
Sessions 8-10 Deepen uncertainty tolerance work, address meta-worry ("worry about worry"), continue behavioral experiments with increasing difficulty
Sessions 11-13 Review progress, address any remaining worry domains, begin spacing sessions
Sessions 14-16 Relapse prevention, create personal "worry management toolkit," final review

⚡ 3. Panic Disorder Panic

Clark's Cognitive Model

Panic attacks are maintained by catastrophic misinterpretation of bodily sensations. The person notices a normal bodily sensation, interprets it as dangerous, which creates more anxiety, which creates more sensations — a rapid escalating loop:

Bodily Sensation
e.g., heart racing
➜
Catastrophic Interpretation
"I'm having a heart attack"
➜
More Anxiety
fight/flight intensifies
➜
More Sensations
dizzy, sweating, chest tight

Treatment Components

1. Psychoeducation About Panic

Key messages to deliver to the client:

  • Panic attacks are the fight-or-flight response firing at the wrong time — they are your body's alarm system, not a medical emergency
  • Heart racing, dizziness, breathlessness, tingling — all are normal adrenaline effects, not signs of heart attack, stroke, or losing control
  • A panic attack cannot kill you, make you faint (blood pressure goes UP in panic, not down), or make you go crazy
  • Panic attacks always peak and pass — usually within 10-15 minutes

2. Cognitive Restructuring

Target the catastrophic misinterpretations directly:

Therapist: "You said when your heart races, you think 'I'm having a heart attack.' What's the evidence for that interpretation?"

Client: "Well... my heart is pounding really fast."

Therapist: "Your heart also pounds when you exercise, right? Or when you watch a scary movie? Has that ever been a heart attack?"

Client: "No..."

Therapist: "And you've had, what — maybe 50 panic attacks? And how many heart attacks?"

Client: "Zero."

Therapist: "So we have 50 data points that say: heart racing during anxiety ≠ heart attack. What's the alternative explanation for why your heart races?"

3. Interoceptive Exposure

The most powerful component: deliberately induce the feared bodily sensations in a controlled setting to prove they are not dangerous.

Exercise Induces Tests the Fear
Spinning in a chair for 60 seconds Dizziness, disorientation "Dizziness means I'll faint/lose control"
Hyperventilation for 60 seconds Lightheadedness, tingling, breathlessness "I can't breathe / I'll suffocate"
Breathing through a thin straw for 2 min Air hunger, chest tightness "Chest tightness = heart problem"
Running in place for 2 minutes Heart racing, sweating, breathlessness "Fast heart = heart attack"
Tensing whole body for 60 seconds Muscle tension, trembling "Tension means something is wrong"

Clinical Tip: Always do interoceptive exercises yourself first, in session, to model that they are safe. Then do them together with the client. Never assign them as homework first.

4. In Vivo Exposure

Gradually face situations that have been avoided due to fear of panic: supermarkets, public transport, elevators, driving, crowded places. Use a graded hierarchy (0-100 fear rating).

5. Dropping Safety Behaviors

Identify and systematically remove:

  • Carrying anti-anxiety medication "just in case"
  • Always being near an exit
  • Having a "safe person" present
  • Checking pulse or blood pressure
  • Distraction techniques (these prevent full processing)

Session Outline (12 Sessions)

Panic Disorder Treatment Session Outline
Sessions 1-2 Assessment, psychoeducation about panic and fight/flight, build individualized Clark's model for THIS client, begin panic diary
Sessions 3-4 Cognitive restructuring of catastrophic misinterpretations, generate alternative explanations for bodily sensations
Sessions 5-7 Interoceptive exposure (in-session first, then homework), begin identifying safety behaviors
Sessions 8-10 In vivo exposure to avoided situations, drop safety behaviors systematically, behavioral experiments
Sessions 11-12 Consolidation, relapse prevention, create personal panic management plan

👥 4. Social Anxiety Disorder Social Anxiety

Clark & Wells Model

Social anxiety is maintained by a unique combination of cognitive processes:

  1. Negative predictions about social situations ("I'll say something stupid, everyone will judge me")
  2. Self-focused attention — in social situations, attention shifts inward (monitoring own behavior, sensations, "how do I look?") instead of outward (what is actually happening)
  3. A distorted self-image — the person "sees" themselves from an observer's perspective as awkward, blushing, trembling (often far worse than reality)
  4. Safety behaviors — rehearsing what to say, avoiding eye contact, speaking quickly
  5. Post-event rumination — after social events, replaying "failures" in detail, reinforcing negative beliefs

Treatment Components

1. Shifting Self-Focused Attention

Therapist: "Right now, I want you to focus entirely on yourself — notice your body, your face, how you're sitting, what you look like. Do this for 30 seconds."

(30 seconds pass)

Therapist: "Now shift. Focus entirely outward — what color is my shirt? What's on the wall behind me? What sounds can you hear? Do this for 30 seconds."

(30 seconds pass)

Therapist: "Which felt more anxious?"

Client: "Definitely the first one."

Therapist: "This is what happens in social situations. When you focus inward, anxiety goes up. When you focus outward — on the other person, the conversation content — anxiety drops. This is a skill we're going to practice."

2. Video Feedback

One of the most powerful techniques for social anxiety:

  1. Ask the client: "How do you think you looked in that conversation?" (they typically describe themselves as visibly anxious, awkward, red-faced)
  2. Record a brief in-session conversation on video
  3. Watch the video together
  4. Clients are almost always shocked: "I look so much more normal than I thought!"

Key principle: Social anxiety creates a distorted internal self-image that is far worse than how the person actually appears to others. Video feedback provides direct, undeniable evidence to correct this distortion.

3. Behavioral Experiments

Test specific predictions without using safety behaviors:

Example: Testing "People will notice I'm anxious and judge me"

Prediction: "If I give my presentation without rehearsing every word, I'll stumble, blush, and everyone will think I'm incompetent."

Experiment: Give the presentation using only bullet points (no script). Focus attention outward on the audience. Notice what actually happens.

Outcome: Stumbled once — audience didn't react. Three people asked good follow-up questions. Manager said "good overview."

Learning: People are focused on the content, not on my anxiety. One stumble is not a catastrophe. I performed better with notes than with a rigid script.

4. Survey Methods

Ask other people what they actually notice and think — this provides external data to counteract the distorted internal image:

  • "Do you ever notice when someone in a meeting seems nervous?"
  • "What would you think if someone blushed during a conversation?"
  • "Have you ever noticed me blushing or looking anxious?"

Results are almost always surprising: people notice far less than the anxious person assumes, and when they do notice nervousness, they view it sympathetically rather than judgmentally.

5. Dropping Safety Behaviors One by One

Safety Behavior What to Drop What to Learn
Rehearsing what to say Speak spontaneously "I can be okay without a script"
Avoiding eye contact Make natural eye contact "People don't react negatively to my gaze"
Speaking quickly to "get it over with" Slow down deliberately "Speaking slowly is actually more effective"
Hiding hands (to conceal trembling) Use hand gestures openly "Nobody is looking at my hands"
Arriving early to "scope out" the room Arrive at normal time "I can handle uncertainty about the setting"

Session Outline (14-16 Sessions)

Social Anxiety Treatment Session Outline
Sessions 1-2 Assessment (LSAS / SPIN), build individualized Clark & Wells model, identify safety behaviors, demonstrate self-focused attention exercise
Sessions 3-5 Attention training (external focus), video feedback, cognitive restructuring of predictions about social evaluation
Sessions 6-9 Behavioral experiments dropping safety behaviors one by one (start with easier situations), survey methods, in-session role plays
Sessions 10-12 More challenging behavioral experiments, address post-event rumination (scheduled review vs. endless replay), work on underlying beliefs about self
Sessions 13-14 Consolidation, review all gains, address remaining avoidance
Sessions 15-16 Relapse prevention, create ongoing exposure plan, final review

🐘 5. Specific Phobias Phobias

Simple Model

Specific phobias have the simplest maintenance model of all anxiety disorders:

Feared Stimulus
e.g., elevator, spider, dog
➜
Immediate Anxiety / Fear
fight/flight activation
➜
Avoidance / Escape
"I'll take the stairs"
➜
Fear Maintained
never learned it was safe

Treatment: Graded Exposure Hierarchy

Specific phobias are among the most treatable conditions in all of mental health. Treatment can often be completed in 1-5 sessions.

  1. Build a fear hierarchy (0-100 scale) — list situations from least to most feared
  2. Start at a manageable level (aim for 40-50/100 anxiety) and stay until anxiety drops by at least 50%
  3. Progress up the hierarchy as each level becomes manageable
  4. Practice in multiple contexts to generalize the learning

Worked Example: Elevator Phobia

Full exposure hierarchy for elevator phobia
Step Task Fear Rating
1 Stand near an elevator and watch others use it 20/100
2 Stand inside an elevator with doors open (don't ride) 35/100
3 Ride one floor up with the therapist 50/100
4 Ride three floors with the therapist 60/100
5 Ride one floor alone 70/100
6 Ride to the top floor alone 80/100
7 Ride in a crowded elevator 85/100
8 Ride in an unfamiliar building's elevator alone 90/100

Outcome: After working through this hierarchy over 3 sessions (with homework practice between sessions), the client reported that elevator anxiety had dropped from 90/100 to 15/100. They now use elevators daily without significant distress.

Modern approach — Inhibitory Learning: Rather than waiting for fear to habituate, the goal is violation of expectancy. Ask: "What do you predict will happen?" Then do the exposure. After: "What actually happened?" The mismatch between prediction and reality is what drives new learning.

🔗 6. Transdiagnostic Principles Across All Anxiety Disorders

What All Anxiety Disorders Share

  • Cognitive distortions about threat — overestimation of danger, underestimation of coping
  • Avoidance — behavioral and/or cognitive
  • Safety behaviors — subtle avoidance that prevents disconfirmation

Universal Treatment Ingredients

  1. Psychoeducation — explain the maintenance model
  2. Cognitive restructuring — identify and challenge threat-based thinking
  3. Behavioral experiments — test predictions in real life
  4. Exposure — face feared situations/sensations
  5. Dropping safety behaviors — remove subtle avoidance

Comparison Table: Key Differences

Feature GAD Panic Social Anxiety Specific Phobia
Core Trigger Uncertainty about future Bodily sensations Social evaluation Specific object/situation
Core Fear "I can't handle not knowing" "These sensations mean danger" "Others will judge me negatively" "That thing/situation is dangerous"
Key Cognitive Process Intolerance of uncertainty, worry as coping Catastrophic misinterpretation of sensations Self-focused attention, distorted self-image Threat overestimation of specific stimulus
Main Intervention Uncertainty tolerance + worry management Interoceptive exposure + cognitive restructuring Attention shift + video feedback + behavioral experiments Graded exposure hierarchy
Typical Duration 12-16 sessions 12 sessions 14-16 sessions 1-5 sessions
Safety Behavior Examples Reassurance-seeking, excessive checking, over-planning Carrying medication, staying near exits, body checking Rehearsing, avoiding eye contact, speaking quietly Complete avoidance of stimulus
Clinical Decision Guide: Which protocol to use?

If the main complaint is...

  • "I worry about everything all the time" → GAD protocol
  • "I get sudden intense attacks out of nowhere and I'm afraid I'll die/lose control" → Panic protocol
  • "I'm terrified of being judged in social situations" → Social Anxiety protocol
  • "I'm afraid of [specific thing] and it's limiting my life" → Specific Phobia protocol
  • "I have multiple anxiety problems" → Start with the most impairing, use transdiagnostic principles, or consider unified protocol (Barlow's UP)

📝 Final Scored Exam

8 multiple-choice questions covering anxiety disorder protocols and treatment principles. Score: 7-8 Excellent, 5-6 Good, 0-4 Needs Review.

1. What is the core mechanism that maintains ALL anxiety disorders according to the CBT model?

Correct! Avoidance is the key maintenance factor across all anxiety disorders — it prevents the person from learning that the feared outcome doesn't happen (or that they could cope).
The answer is B. While biology may contribute to anxiety vulnerability, the CBT model identifies avoidance as the core maintenance mechanism — it blocks the corrective learning that would reduce fear.

2. In Dugas' model, what is the core problem in Generalized Anxiety Disorder?

Correct! GAD is fundamentally driven by intolerance of uncertainty — the belief that not knowing is unbearable and must be resolved through worry.
The answer is C. Dugas' model identifies intolerance of uncertainty as the core vulnerability in GAD. Options B and D describe panic and social anxiety respectively.

3. A client with panic disorder says "When my heart races, I know I'm about to have a heart attack." According to Clark's model, this is an example of:

Correct! This is the hallmark of Clark's model — a normal bodily sensation (heart racing) is misinterpreted as catastrophically dangerous (heart attack), fueling the panic cycle.
The answer is C. Clark's cognitive model of panic centers on catastrophic misinterpretation — interpreting normal fight/flight sensations as signs of imminent medical catastrophe.

4. What is the purpose of interoceptive exposure in treating panic disorder?

Correct! Interoceptive exposure (spinning, hyperventilation, etc.) deliberately creates the feared sensations in a controlled setting, demonstrating that these sensations are uncomfortable but not dangerous.
The answer is B. Interoceptive exposure deliberately induces the feared sensations (dizziness, heart racing, breathlessness) through exercises like spinning or hyperventilation — proving that the sensations themselves are harmless. Option C describes in vivo exposure, which is a different component.

5. In Clark & Wells' model of social anxiety, what unique cognitive process maintains the disorder?

Correct! In social anxiety, the person shifts attention inward, monitoring their own appearance and behavior, which creates an exaggeratedly negative self-image and increases anxiety — a uniquely self-focused maintenance process.
The answer is B. Clark & Wells identified self-focused attention as the distinctive maintenance factor in social anxiety — the person "watches" themselves from the outside, creating a distorted image that is far more negative than how they actually appear.

6. A client with social anxiety insists they "look visibly anxious and everyone can tell." What is the most effective technique to address this specific belief?

Correct! Video feedback provides direct, visual evidence that the person looks far more normal than their distorted internal self-image suggests. It's one of the most powerful techniques for social anxiety.
The answer is C. Video feedback directly addresses the distorted self-image by showing the client how they actually appear — which is almost always far better than they imagined. Thought records can help, but video provides undeniable visual evidence.

7. Which anxiety disorder typically requires the fewest treatment sessions?

Correct! Specific phobias are among the most treatable conditions in mental health. With a graded exposure hierarchy, significant improvement can occur in as few as 1-5 sessions.
The answer is D. Specific phobias have the simplest maintenance model (stimulus → avoidance → maintained fear) and respond remarkably well to graded exposure — often 1-5 sessions is sufficient.

8. A client worries constantly about "what if" scenarios (job loss, health crises, family emergencies) but currently has no real problems. The worry is unproductive and goes in circles. What is the most appropriate intervention?

Correct! For unproductive "what if" worries (GAD), worry postponement contains the worry to a set time, and imaginal exposure helps the person face and process the feared scenario rather than endlessly circling it.
The answer is D. This is a classic GAD presentation with unproductive worry. Worry postponement limits worry to a set daily time, and imaginal exposure (writing out and re-reading the worst-case scenario) helps process the fear rather than avoiding it through endless worry.
