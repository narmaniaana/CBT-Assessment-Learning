⚠️ Common Misconception — Let's Correct This First

Many trainees describe Clark's panic model as being about agoraphobia and avoidance. This is incorrect. Avoidance/agoraphobia is a secondary consequence, not the core mechanism. The PRIMARY mechanism is the catastrophic misinterpretation of bodily sensations. Keep this front and center as you study this lesson.

🩸 A. Clark's Cognitive Model of Panic Disorder (1986)

The Core Mechanism

Clark's model rests on one elegant insight: panic attacks are caused by the catastrophic misinterpretation of normal bodily sensations. The person doesn't just feel anxious — they interpret their body's normal anxiety response as evidence of imminent danger.

💡 The Key Insight

A racing heart is not dangerous. But if you interpret a racing heart as meaning "I'm having a heart attack," your brain responds to that interpretation with more fear, which produces MORE physical sensations, which produces MORE catastrophic interpretation — creating a self-amplifying spiral that peaks as a full panic attack.

Typical catastrophic misinterpretations:

  • Heart racing → "I'm having a heart attack"
  • Dizziness → "I'm going to faint / I have a brain tumor"
  • Breathlessness → "I'm suffocating / I'm going to stop breathing"
  • Derealization → "I'm going crazy / losing my mind"
  • Trembling/tingling → "I'm having a stroke"

💥 FULL PANIC ATTACK

Why Avoidance & Agoraphobia Develop (Secondary, NOT Primary)

After repeated panic attacks, the person begins to avoid situations where they've panicked before — supermarkets, public transport, crowded places. They may also develop safety behaviors (carrying water, sitting near exits, always having their phone). But these are consequences of the catastrophic misinterpretation cycle, not the core mechanism itself.

This distinction is clinically critical: if you only treat avoidance (through graded exposure), you may reduce avoidance but leave the core misinterpretation intact. The person remains vulnerable to future panic because they still believe their bodily sensations are dangerous.

Key Treatment Targets

1. Behavioral Experiments (Interoceptive Exposure)

Deliberately induce the feared sensations in a safe setting to show they are not dangerous:

  • Spinning in a chair → induces dizziness → tests "dizziness means I'll faint"
  • Hyperventilation (60 seconds) → induces breathlessness, tingling → tests "I'll suffocate"
  • Running up stairs / jumping jacks → induces heart racing → tests "heart racing = heart attack"
  • Breathing through a straw → induces breathlessness → tests "I can't breathe"
  • Staring at a spot on the wall → induces derealization → tests "I'm going crazy"

2. Cognitive Restructuring of Misinterpretations

Directly challenge the catastrophic interpretation: "What is the evidence that a racing heart means a heart attack? Has your heart ever stopped? What did the cardiologist say?"

3. Dropping Safety Behaviors

Gradually eliminate: carrying water bottles "just in case," sitting near exits, checking pulse, calling someone when anxious. Each safety behavior maintains the belief that danger was averted because of the behavior, not because there was never any danger.

👩
Case Study: Mariam, 31
Panic attacks in supermarkets | Referred by GP after cardiac clearance

Presenting problem: Mariam has had 4 panic attacks in the last 2 months, all in or near supermarkets. She now avoids large stores and only shops at a small corner store near her home. She carries a water bottle everywhere and keeps her phone in hand "in case I need to call an ambulance."

What she reports: "When I'm in the supermarket, I start to feel my heart beating fast. Then I get hot and dizzy. I feel like I'm going to have a heart attack or collapse. Last time I had to leave and sit on the curb. I thought I was dying."

🔍 Formulation: Mapping Mariam's Cycle

⚡ Trigger: Enters supermarket (bright lights, noise, heat)
↓
Perceived threat: "This doesn't feel right. Last time I panicked here."
↓
Apprehension: Anxiety activates. She scans her body for signs.
↓
Bodily sensations: Heart racing, feeling hot, dizziness
↓
🚨 Misinterpretation: "I'm having a heart attack. I'm going to collapse."
↓
Safety behaviors: Grips phone, drinks water, moves toward exit
↓
Escapes → Relief → Believes: "If I hadn't left, I WOULD have had a heart attack"

🛠️ Treatment Plan for Mariam

1
Psychoeducation: Explain the panic cycle. Share the Clark model. Normalize bodily sensations. Review cardiac clearance results — her heart is healthy.
2
Cognitive Restructuring: Identify and challenge "heart racing = heart attack." Evidence for and against. What does a heart attack actually feel like vs. anxiety? Has she ever had a heart attack? What happened when she didn't escape?
3
Behavioral Experiments (Interoceptive): In session: run up stairs together (induce heart racing). Rate catastrophe prediction before (0-100%). Do the experiment. Rate after. "Did you have a heart attack?" Repeat with jumping jacks, spinning.
4
Drop Safety Behaviors: Leave phone in bag. Don't carry water bottle. Stand in the middle of the store, not near the exit. Don't check pulse.
5
Graded Return to Supermarket: Visit store at quiet time → busier times → stay longer → without safety behaviors. Each visit is a behavioral experiment testing: "Will I have a heart attack?"

🗣️ B. Clark & Wells' Model of Social Anxiety (1995)

The Three Maintaining Mechanisms

Clark and Wells identified three processes that keep social anxiety going. Unlike panic (which centers on bodily misinterpretation), social anxiety is maintained by a distorted processing of the social self.

👁 1. Self-Focused Attention

Instead of processing external social cues (what others actually say and do), the socially anxious person turns attention inward: monitoring their own blushing, voice trembling, sweating, "looking stupid." They construct an internal observer's image of how they think they appear — and it's almost always distorted and negative.

Result: They miss positive social feedback, perform worse because they're not fully present, and come away "confirming" they did badly.

🛡️ 2. Safety Behaviors

Actions designed to prevent the feared social catastrophe: rehearsing what to say, avoiding eye contact, speaking quickly, gripping objects to hide trembling, wearing heavy makeup to hide blushing, overpreparing presentations.

Result: These behaviors make the person seem stiff, distant, or disengaged — ironically creating the negative impression they feared. They also prevent disconfirmation: "The meeting went okay, but ONLY because I rehearsed every word."

🔄 3. Post-Event Rumination

After the social situation, the person replays the interaction in detail — but with a negative bias. They focus on moments they felt anxious, things they said "wrong," any ambiguous reactions from others. Over hours or days, the memory becomes increasingly negative, cementing the belief that they performed terribly.

Result: Even neutral or positive interactions get rewritten as failures, increasing avoidance of future situations.

Key Treatment Approaches

  • Attention Training: Shift from internal monitoring to external focus. Practice: "What color are their eyes? What did they actually say?"
  • Video Feedback: Record the person in a social interaction. They predict how they looked (anxious, shaky, blushing). Then watch the video — they almost always look much more normal than expected. Powerful disconfirmation.
  • Dropping Safety Behaviors: Conduct same social situation with and without safety behaviors. Compare outcomes.
  • Behavioral Experiments: "If I make deliberate eye contact, people will look disgusted" → Test it → Record actual reactions.
  • Survey Methods: Ask trusted others: "When someone blushes, what do you think?" Most people say "it's normal" or "it's endearing" — not "they're incompetent."
👨
Case Study: Davit, 26
Software developer | Avoids team meetings | Performance at risk

Presenting problem: Davit dreads weekly team meetings. He spends hours the night before rehearsing what he might say. During meetings, he barely speaks. When he does, he monitors himself intensely — his voice, his facial expression, whether his hands shake. Afterward, he spends the rest of the day replaying everything he said, convinced he sounded "stupid and incompetent."

Core beliefs: "I'm not as smart as my colleagues." "If they see the real me, they'll realize I don't deserve this job."

🔍 Formulation: Mapping Davit's Cycle

👥 Trigger: Weekly team meeting
↓
Beliefs activate: "I'm not smart enough. They'll see I'm a fraud."
↓
Perceived danger: "If I speak, I'll sound stupid. They'll judge me."
↓
👁 Self-focus: Monitors voice, hands, face
🛡️ Safety: Rehearses, avoids eye contact, stays quiet
↓
Symptoms: Mind goes blank, voice shakes when he speaks, sweating
↓
Outcome: Barely participates → Colleagues don't engage with him → "Confirms" he's not valued
↓

🛠️ Treatment Plan for Davit

1
Psychoeducation: Share the Clark & Wells model. Show how self-focus + safety behaviors + rumination create the problem. Davit's "evidence" that he's incompetent is manufactured by the cycle.
2
Video Feedback: Role-play a brief presentation. Davit predicts: "I'll look terrified, shaky, red-faced." Record and watch. Typically: he looks far more composed than he feels.
3
Attention Shifting: In next meeting, focus externally: notice what others say, what color shirt the speaker wears. Rate anxiety with internal vs. external focus. Compare.
4
Drop Safety Behaviors: Attend meeting without rehearsing. Speak at least once without pre-planning. Make eye contact. Don't hold objects to hide hands.
5
Behavioral Experiment: Prediction: "If I share an idea without rehearsing, people will think it's stupid." Experiment: Share one spontaneous idea. Record actual reactions. Re-rate belief.
6
Survey: Ask 2-3 trusted colleagues: "When someone takes a moment to find words in a meeting, what do you think?" Collect actual data.

🔬 C. Dugas' Intolerance of Uncertainty Model of GAD (1998)

The Core Vulnerability: Intolerance of Uncertainty (IU)

Dugas proposed that the central feature of Generalized Anxiety Disorder is not worry per se, but an underlying intolerance of uncertainty — a dispositional tendency to find uncertain situations unacceptable, regardless of how likely a negative outcome actually is.

The person with GAD doesn't just worry more — they cannot tolerate not knowing. "What if something goes wrong?" is not a question to answer; it's a state they cannot sit with.

💡 Key Distinction: GAD vs. Normal Worry

Everyone worries. But in GAD, worry is triggered by uncertainty itself, not by the probability of a bad outcome. A person with GAD might worry equally about a 1% chance and a 50% chance — because both involve not knowing for sure.

The Four Maintaining Factors

⚡ 1. Intolerance of Uncertainty

The core: "I need to know for sure." "What if?" thinking. Even small uncertainties feel threatening. This drives all the worry.

👍 2. Positive Beliefs About Worry

"Worrying helps me prepare." "If I worry, I won't be caught off guard." "Worry shows I care." These beliefs make the person reluctant to stop worrying — it feels protective.

🚫 3. Negative Problem Orientation

"Problems are threats, not challenges." "I can't cope." "Things will go badly." This makes the person freeze instead of problem-solve, which creates more unresolved issues to worry about.

😶 4. Cognitive Avoidance

Paradoxically, worry itself is a form of avoidance — it's verbal-linguistic (abstract "what if" chains) rather than imaginal (vivid emotional imagery). This keeps the person from fully processing their fear. They worry in words to avoid feeling the emotion.

Key Treatment Approaches

  • Behavioral Experiments Targeting IU: Deliberately introduce small uncertainties and observe the outcome. "Go to a restaurant without reading reviews first." "Send an email without proofreading 5 times." "Don't check child's location for 2 hours."
  • Worry Postponement: Set a daily 20-minute "worry time." Outside of it, notice worry and postpone it. This tests the belief "If I don't worry now, something bad will happen."
  • Challenging Positive Beliefs About Worry: "Has worrying ever actually prevented a bad outcome?" "Does worrying prepare you, or just exhaust you?" Examine evidence.
  • Problem-Solving Training: Distinguish solvable problems from hypothetical worries. For solvable problems: define, brainstorm, choose, act. For hypothetical: accept uncertainty.
  • Imaginal Exposure: Write out the worst-case scenario in vivid detail. Read it repeatedly until anxiety habituates. This replaces abstract worry with direct emotional processing.
👩
Case Study: Nino, 38
Teacher and mother of two | Constant worry about "everything" | Chronic muscle tension, poor sleep

Presenting problem: Nino describes herself as "always worrying." She worries about her children's health, her husband's job security, her students' performance, the economy, her own health. She checks her children's location app constantly. She re-reads every email 4-5 times before sending. She lies awake at night running through "what if" scenarios.

Key cognitions: "If I stop worrying, something bad will happen and it'll be my fault." "I just need to be prepared." "What if my daughter gets sick and I didn't notice the signs?"

🔍 Formulation: Mapping Nino's Model

🔐 IU: Nino cannot tolerate any uncertainty about her family's safety or future
↓
❓ Trigger: Daughter is 10 minutes late from school
↓
👍 Positive belief: "If I worry and plan, I can keep everyone safe"
↓
Worry chain: "What if there was an accident? → What if she's in hospital? → What if I can't reach her? → What if she's seriously hurt?"
↓
🚫 "I can't cope if something happened"
😶 Checks location app 6x. Calls twice.
↓
Daughter arrives home safe → "See, it's because I was vigilant" → Worry cycle reinforced

🛠️ Treatment Plan for Nino

1
Psychoeducation: Introduce the IU model. Help Nino see that worry is not protective — it's a response to intolerance of uncertainty, not actual danger.
2
Challenge Positive Beliefs: "How many times has worrying prevented something bad? Does the worry stop when you get reassurance, or does it shift to the next topic?" Track worry outcomes for 1 week.
3
IU Behavioral Experiments: Don't check daughter's location app for 1 hour. Send email without re-reading. Leave house without double-checking locks. Rate prediction vs. outcome.
4
Worry Postponement: Set 6:00 PM as worry time (20 minutes). When worry arises during the day, note it and postpone. By evening, most worries have resolved themselves.
5
Problem-Solving Training: Sort worries into solvable vs. hypothetical. "Daughter's homework" = solvable (help her tonight). "What if she gets sick in the future" = hypothetical (practice accepting uncertainty).
6
Imaginal Exposure: Write the worst-case scenario in full: "My daughter is seriously ill and I can't help." Read it daily until anxiety decreases. Process the fear fully instead of avoiding it through abstract worry.

Exercise 1: Map the Cycle

Read the following vignette and draw the full cycle on paper using the appropriate model.

Luka, 24, feels his heart pound whenever he exercises at the gym. He immediately stops, sits down, and checks his pulse. He's started avoiding the gym entirely. He told his doctor: "I'm afraid my heart will give out."

💡 Which model? Click to reveal
Clark's Panic Model. Luka is catastrophically misinterpreting a normal bodily sensation (heart pounding from exercise) as meaning something dangerous ("my heart will give out"). His avoidance of the gym is secondary to the misinterpretation. Treatment: interoceptive exposure (deliberately raising heart rate in session), cognitive restructuring of the misinterpretation, and returning to the gym as a behavioral experiment.

Exercise 2: Identify the Maintaining Mechanisms

Tamta, 29, had a work presentation yesterday. She spent 3 hours rehearsing exactly what to say. During the presentation, she was focused on controlling her voice and checking if her hands were shaking. Afterward, she replayed the whole thing in her mind and concluded "everyone could see how nervous I was." She's now dreading the next one.

💡 Identify all three maintaining mechanisms
Clark & Wells' Social Anxiety Model.
1. Safety behavior: Rehearsing for 3 hours (prevents discovering she can speak spontaneously).
2. Self-focused attention: Monitoring her voice and hands during the presentation instead of attending to the audience.
3. Post-event rumination: Replaying the presentation afterward with a negative bias ("everyone could see how nervous I was").
All three mechanisms worked together to maintain her social anxiety despite the presentation objectively going fine.

Exercise 3: Design an Intervention

Your client with GAD says: "I check the news 15 times a day because what if something happens and I'm not prepared?" She believes worry keeps her safe.

💡 Design two interventions from the Dugas model
1. Challenge Positive Beliefs About Worry: "How many times has checking the news actually prepared you for a real emergency? What would happen if you checked twice instead of 15 times? Let's test it."

2. IU Behavioral Experiment: Reduce news checking to 2x/day for one week. Prediction: "Something bad will happen and I won't be prepared." Record what actually happens. Rate "something bad happened because I didn't check" before and after the week.

Bonus: Worry postponement — when the urge to check arises, note it, postpone to designated worry time.

Reveal Answer
Catastrophic misinterpretation of bodily sensations. NOT avoidance, NOT agoraphobia, NOT "fear of fear." The specific mechanism is interpreting normal bodily sensations (e.g., heart racing) as signs of imminent catastrophe (e.g., "I'm having a heart attack"). Avoidance is a secondary consequence.

3 A client says: "I can't go to the meeting because everyone will see that my hands are trembling and think I'm incompetent." Which model best applies, and what are the specific maintaining mechanisms operating here?

Clark & Wells' Social Anxiety Model.
- Self-focused attention: They're already planning to monitor their trembling
- Safety behavior: Avoidance of the meeting entirely
- Core belief: "Others will judge me as incompetent"
Treatment would include: attending the meeting while shifting to external focus, dropping avoidance, behavioral experiment testing the specific prediction ("If my hands tremble, people will think I'm incompetent").
4 In Dugas' model of GAD, what are the four maintaining factors?
Reveal Answer
1. Intolerance of Uncertainty (IU) — the core: "I need to know for sure"
2. Positive Beliefs About Worry — "Worry helps me prepare / stay safe"
3. Negative Problem Orientation — "Problems are threats, I can't cope"
4. Cognitive Avoidance — Abstract verbal worry replaces emotional processing of feared scenarios
5 Design a behavioral experiment for a panic client who fears that a racing heart means they are having a heart attack.
Reveal Answer
Interoceptive Behavioral Experiment:
Belief to test: "If my heart races, I'm having a heart attack" (Belief: 80%)
Prediction: "If I do jumping jacks for 2 minutes, I'll have a heart attack or collapse"
Experiment: Do 2 minutes of jumping jacks in session with therapist. Note heart rate goes up.
Observe: Heart races. No heart attack. No collapse. Heart rate returns to normal within minutes.
Conclusion: A racing heart is a normal response to exertion, not evidence of a heart attack.
Belief re-rating: Down to 30%
Follow-up: Repeat in different settings. Add: climbing stairs, caffeine. Gradually drop safety behaviors.
6 A client says: "I worry because it helps me prepare for the worst. If I stop worrying, something bad will happen." Which model is this, and how would you challenge this belief?
Reveal Answer
Dugas' GAD Model — specifically "Positive Beliefs About Worry."

Challenge strategies:
1. Evidence review: "Can you think of a time you worried and the bad thing happened anyway? A time you didn't worry and things were fine?"
2. Behavioral experiment: "Pick one low-stakes situation. Don't worry about it for a week. Did something bad happen because you didn't worry?"
3. Cost-benefit analysis: "What has worrying cost you? (sleep, health, relationships, time). What has it actually prevented?"
4. Logical challenge: "If worry prevents bad things, shouldn't chronic worriers have the best lives? Do they?"
7 Why does video feedback work in the treatment of social anxiety? What does it target?
Reveal Answer
Video feedback targets the distorted self-image created by self-focused attention.

In social anxiety, the person constructs an internal "observer's view" of themselves that is far more negative than reality — they imagine themselves visibly shaking, blushing, stumbling over words. Video feedback shows them what they actually look like (usually far more composed than they expected). This directly disconfirms the negative self-image and provides powerful evidence against beliefs like "everyone can see how anxious I am." It's essentially a behavioral experiment testing the prediction "I'll look terrible" against objective evidence.
