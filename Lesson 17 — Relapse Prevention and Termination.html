Relapse Prevention & Termination

Ending therapy well and preparing clients to maintain their gains independently

1When Is the Client Ready to End?

Key Principle: In CBT, termination is discussed from Session 1. CBT is explicitly time-limited. Clients should know from the beginning that the goal is to make themselves their own therapist.

Readiness for termination is assessed across four domains:

The Four Readiness Criteria

Criterion What to Look For How to Assess
1. Symptom Improvement Scores in minimal or mild range for 3+ consecutive sessions PHQ-9 ≤ 9, GAD-7 ≤ 9, or equivalent on other measures for at least 3 sessions
2. Skill Competence Client can independently use CBT tools Can complete thought records without help, designs own behavioral experiments, catches and challenges automatic thoughts in real-time
3. Goal Achievement Original therapy goals have been met Review the goals set in Session 1-2. Have they been achieved? (If goals shifted, discuss this explicitly)
4. Self-Efficacy Client feels confident managing independently "On a scale of 0-10, how confident are you that you could handle a difficult period on your own using the skills you've learned?"
Common Trap: Waiting until ALL symptoms are gone. The goal of CBT is not to eliminate all negative emotions — it's to give clients the tools to manage them. A client who still experiences occasional sadness but can use their skills effectively is ready to end.
What if one criterion is met but not the others?
This is very common. For example:

Symptoms improved but skills not strong: Continue therapy with a focus on skill consolidation. Reduce session content and increase in-session practice.

Skills strong but symptoms still elevated: Consider whether there are maintaining factors you haven't addressed, or whether the formulation needs updating. Some clients may benefit from referral for complementary treatment (e.g., medication evaluation).

Goals met but client doesn't feel confident: This may reflect underlying beliefs about being incapable. Address this therapeutically — it might be the most important work you do before ending.

2Spacing Out Sessions

Abrupt endings are bad endings. CBT uses a graduated termination process that systematically tests the client's ability to cope independently while maintaining a safety net.

Weekly Sessions (Active Treatment Phase)

Standard CBT frequency. Therapist is actively teaching skills, client is learning and practicing.

Biweekly Sessions (2 weeks apart)

First step in spacing. Client manages a full week independently. Session focuses on: "How did you handle things on your own? What worked? What was hard?"

Monthly Sessions

Client is now largely independent. Sessions shift to troubleshooting, reinforcing gains, and beginning relapse prevention planning.

Final Session + Booster Plan

Complete the therapy blueprint, celebrate progress, schedule booster sessions. The formal therapeutic relationship transitions to an "as-needed" resource.

Why This Works: Each gap between sessions is a mini-experiment: "Can I cope on my own for two weeks?" If yes, the client builds confidence. If a difficulty arises, it becomes therapeutic material: "Great — you had a hard week. Let's look at how you handled it and what you might do differently next time."
Introducing Spacing to the Client

"We've been making great progress, and I think you're ready to start testing your wings a bit. What I'd like to suggest is that we move to meeting every two weeks instead of every week. This gives you more time to practice on your own, and when we meet, we can look at how things went. Think of it as a trial run for independence. How does that sound?"

What if the client deteriorates during a gap between sessions?
This is not a failure — it's valuable data. Options:

1. If mild deterioration: Use it therapeutically. "What happened? What skills did you try? What could you do differently?" This teaches real-world coping.

2. If significant deterioration: Return temporarily to weekly sessions. This is not a setback — it means the client needs more support at this stage. Re-space when stable again.

3. If crisis: Provide crisis support and reassess the treatment plan entirely. Termination was premature — continue active treatment.

3The Therapy Blueprint (Relapse Prevention Plan)

The therapy blueprint is a concrete document the client takes home — a personalized manual for maintaining their gains and handling future difficulties. It should be written collaboratively over the final 2-3 sessions.

My Therapy Blueprint
Client: Sarah M. | Diagnosis: Major Depressive Disorder | Sessions Completed: 14

1. What I Learned About Myself

  • My core belief is "I'm not good enough" — this developed in childhood when I couldn't meet my parents' high expectations
  • This belief gets activated by criticism, failure, or comparison with others
  • When activated, I withdraw, stop doing activities I enjoy, and my thinking becomes very black-and-white
  • Depression was maintained by avoidance of social activities and rumination — NOT by a permanent character flaw

2. What Techniques Work for Me

  • Thought records: Especially effective when I catch "all-or-nothing" thinking. Writing it down makes the distortion obvious.
  • Behavioral activation: Even when I don't feel like it, walking and calling a friend reliably improve my mood within 30 minutes.
  • Behavioral experiments: Testing my predictions (like "they'll think I'm boring") showed me my predictions are almost always wrong.
  • Activity scheduling: Scheduling pleasurable activities in advance prevents me from canceling when I "don't feel like it."

3. My Early Warning Signs

  • Canceling social plans more than once a week
  • Sleeping more than 9 hours a night for 3+ days
  • Thoughts like "What's the point?" or "Nobody really cares"
  • Stopping my exercise routine
  • Increased irritability with my partner

4. My Action Plan (When I Notice Warning Signs)

  1. Day 1-3: Complete a thought record. Resume my walking routine. Call one friend.
  2. Day 4-7: If warning signs persist, schedule pleasurable activities for each day. Review this blueprint.
  3. Week 2: If not improving, contact my therapist for a booster session.
  4. Immediately: If I have thoughts of self-harm, call the crisis line or go to the emergency room.

5. My Support Network

  • Close friend: Maria (can call anytime, knows about my depression)
  • Family: Brother David (weekly check-in calls)
  • Professional: Dr. Thompson (therapist — can schedule booster session)
  • Crisis: National crisis line, local emergency services

6. When to Return to Therapy

  • PHQ-9 score returns to moderate range (10+) for more than 2 weeks
  • Warning signs persist for 2+ weeks despite using my action plan
  • Major life stressor that I feel unable to cope with alone
  • I want a "tune-up" — there is no shame in returning for a few sessions
Process Note: The blueprint should be written IN the client's words, not the therapist's clinical language. It's THEIR document. Have the client type or write it, with you guiding and adding points they might miss.

4Predicting and Normalizing Setbacks

One of the most important things you do before ending therapy: predict that setbacks will happen and normalize them in advance. This prevents a future bad week from becoming a catastrophe ("Therapy didn't work! I'm back to square one!").

Lapse

A temporary dip in mood or return of some symptoms. Normal. Expected. Time-limited.

Example: A bad week after a stressful event at work. Mood drops, sleep disrupted for a few days.

Relapse

A full return to the pre-treatment level of the disorder. Prolonged. Requires professional intervention.

Example: Persistent depression lasting weeks, inability to function, withdrawal from all activities.

The Key Message: "A setback is not a comeback of the old problem — it's a chance to practice your new skills under pressure. The difference between you now and you before therapy is that you have tools."

The Time Machine Exercise

A powerful relapse prevention technique done in the final session:

Role-Play Script

Therapist: "Let's imagine it's three months from now. You've had a really tough week — maybe something stressful happened at work, or you had an argument with someone close to you. You notice your mood dropping. You start thinking 'Here we go again.' Walk me through what you would do."

Client: [Practices their plan out loud — identifying warning signs, choosing which tools to use, deciding when to seek support]

Therapist: "Excellent. You just demonstrated that you know exactly what to do. That knowledge doesn't disappear — it's yours now."

What if the client says "But what if the skills don't work?"
This is a common and understandable fear. Address it directly:

1. Validate: "That's a normal worry. It shows you take your mental health seriously."

2. Reality-check: "The skills have worked for you over the past [X] weeks. What evidence do you have that they'll suddenly stop working?"

3. Safety net: "And if for some reason you're struggling and the skills aren't enough, you can always call me for a booster session. That's not failure — that's smart self-care."

4. Normalize: "Some situations are genuinely harder than others. No skill works 100% of the time. The goal is to have multiple tools and the wisdom to know when to ask for help."

5Booster Sessions

Booster sessions are scheduled follow-up appointments after formal therapy has ended. They are one of the most effective relapse prevention strategies available.

Typical Booster Schedule

Timing Purpose Focus
1 month after final session Check early independence How are skills holding up? Any emerging difficulties? Review blueprint.
3 months after final session Reinforce gains Troubleshoot any challenges. Practice skills that have become rusty. Adjust action plan if needed.
6 months after final session Long-term maintenance check Celebrate sustained gains. Address any new life stressors. Final measures for comparison.
What Happens in a Booster Session:
  1. Administer outcome measures (PHQ-9, GAD-7) — compare to end-of-treatment scores
  2. Review: "What's been going well? What's been challenging?"
  3. Check skill use: "Which tools have you been using? Any you've stopped using?"
  4. Troubleshoot any difficulties
  5. Update the therapy blueprint if needed
  6. Reinforce confidence: "You've managed [X months] independently — that's evidence of your capability"
Research Support: Studies show that booster sessions reduce relapse rates by up to 50% compared to no follow-up. They are especially valuable for depression, where relapse rates are naturally high (up to 50% within 2 years).
What if the client needs more than a booster?
If a booster session reveals significant deterioration, transition back to regular therapy:

- Mild difficulties: One or two additional sessions may be enough to get back on track.
- Moderate difficulties: A short course of 4-6 sessions focused on the specific issue.
- Significant relapse: Full course of treatment may be needed. This is NOT a failure — it may reflect a new stressor, life change, or an issue that wasn't fully resolved initially.

Frame returning to therapy positively: "You recognized the problem early and sought help quickly. That's exactly what your blueprint told you to do."

6Common Termination Challenges

Challenge 1: Client Doesn't Want to End (Dependency)

Therapist Script

"I understand the idea of ending feels uncomfortable. That's natural — we've built a strong working relationship. But I want you to notice something: the progress you've made isn't because of me. It's because YOU did the work. I taught you the tools, but you're the one who used them every day. You already have everything you need."

  • Normalize the anxiety about ending
  • Point to specific evidence of the client's competence
  • Remind them about booster sessions as a safety net
  • Consider whether dependency reflects an underlying belief ("I can't cope alone") that needs to be addressed before ending

Challenge 2: Client Wants to End Prematurely

Therapist Script

"I'm glad you're feeling better — that's great progress. I want to make sure we don't stop too early though. Sometimes when symptoms improve, it can feel like everything is fixed. But we haven't yet built the skills to prevent the problem from coming back. Can we discuss the pros and cons of stopping now versus having a few more sessions to solidify your gains?"

  • Validate the improvement while expressing concern
  • Use Socratic dialogue — don't lecture or argue
  • If they insist, respect their autonomy but complete a blueprint and offer booster sessions
  • Explore whether avoidance is driving the decision (avoiding difficult work)

Challenge 3: Therapist's Own Feelings About Ending

  • It's normal to feel sad, worried, or even reluctant to end with a client you've worked closely with
  • Watch for over-treating: continuing therapy because YOU enjoy the work, not because the client needs it
  • Seek supervision if you're unsure whether to terminate
  • The best measure of your success is a client who no longer needs you

Challenge 4: Unresolved Issues at Termination

  • Not every problem can be solved in one course of therapy — and that's okay
  • Prioritize: which issues are most important to address before ending?
  • For remaining issues: include them in the blueprint as areas for continued self-work
  • Consider referral for issues outside your expertise (e.g., couples therapy, substance use treatment)
  • Create a specific plan: "For the self-esteem work, you might want to read [specific book] and practice the exercises we discussed"
The final session: What does it look like?
A final CBT session typically includes:

1. Final measures: PHQ-9, GAD-7, and any disorder-specific measures. Compare to intake scores.

2. Review of progress: Look at the full score trajectory. Celebrate the change. "When we started, your PHQ-9 was 21. Today it's 4. You did that."

3. Complete the blueprint: If not already done, finalize the relapse prevention plan together.

4. Time machine exercise: Role-play a future difficult scenario to practice coping skills.

5. Schedule boosters: Book 1-3 follow-up sessions.

6. Acknowledge the ending: "It's been a privilege working with you. I'm confident in your ability to handle what comes next."

7. Leave the door open: "If you ever need a tune-up session, you're always welcome to reach out."
