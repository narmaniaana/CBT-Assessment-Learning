Lesson 4

Core Beliefs & Intermediate Beliefs

Understanding the deepest levels of cognition, how they form, how they filter experience, and how to modify them in therapy

~30 min study time 8-question final exam
Section 1

The Three Levels of Cognition

CBT identifies three interconnected levels of thinking. Understanding this hierarchy is essential before you can work effectively with deeper cognitions.

The Cognitive Iceberg
Automatic Thoughts
Surface level, situation-specific, easiest to access
"She didn't reply to my text. She must be angry at me."
Intermediate Beliefs
Rules, assumptions, and attitudes that guide behavior
"If someone doesn't respond quickly, it means they don't care."
▼
Core Beliefs
Deepest, unconditional, global, formed in childhood
"I am unlovable."

Summary of the Three Levels

Level Characteristics Example
Automatic Thoughts Situation-specific, stream-of-consciousness, relatively easy to identify and modify "I'll probably mess up this presentation"
Intermediate Beliefs Cross-situational rules and assumptions, often conditional ("If...then"), guide behavior patterns "If I don't perform perfectly, people will lose respect for me"
Core Beliefs Global, unconditional, rigid, felt as absolute truth, resistant to change "I am incompetent"
Test Yourself: Which level is this? "It's terrible to show weakness in front of others."

Answer: Intermediate belief (attitude).

This is an attitude — a value judgment that acts as a general rule guiding behavior. It is not situation-specific (so it's not an automatic thought), and it's not unconditional enough to be a core belief. It sits in the middle layer, shaping how automatic thoughts are generated.

Section 2

Core Beliefs in Detail

Key Characteristics

  • Unconditional: Not dependent on any particular situation ("I am incompetent" vs. "I did badly on that test")
  • Global: Apply across all areas of life, not just one domain
  • Rigid: Experienced as absolute, black-and-white facts about oneself, others, or the world
  • Felt as truth: Clients do not experience them as beliefs — they feel them as reality
  • Formed early: Usually originate in childhood through significant relationships and experiences

Beck's Three Categories of Negative Core Beliefs

⚠

Helplessness

  • "I am incompetent"
  • "I am powerless"
  • "I am weak"
  • "I can't cope"
  • "I am trapped"
❤

Unlovability

  • "I am unlovable"
  • "I am unwanted"
  • "I am defective"
  • "I am bound to be rejected"
  • "I am alone"
★

Worthlessness

  • "I am worthless"
  • "I am useless"
  • "I don't matter"
  • "I am a burden"
  • "I am insignificant"

How Core Beliefs Form

Core beliefs develop through the interaction of childhood experiences, significant events, and repeated patterns. A child who is consistently criticized by a parent may form the belief "I am incompetent." A child who experiences neglect may internalize "I am unlovable." These beliefs are not the result of a single event — they are typically shaped by a pattern of experiences that coalesce into a fundamental schema about oneself.

Core Beliefs as Filters: The Confirmation Bias Engine

Key concept: Once formed, core beliefs act as perceptual filters. The individual notices, encodes, and remembers information that confirms the belief while systematically discounting or ignoring information that contradicts it. This is why core beliefs are so resistant to change — the client genuinely does not "see" contradicting evidence.

Clinical Examples: Three Clients, Three Filters

Client A — Core Belief: "I am incompetent" (Helplessness)

Situation: Receives performance review at work with mostly positive feedback and one area for improvement.

What she notices: Fixates entirely on the one criticism. "See? I knew I wasn't good enough."

What she ignores: The five areas rated "exceeds expectations." When the therapist points them out, she says "They were just being nice."

Client B — Core Belief: "I am unlovable" (Unlovability)

Situation: Friend cancels dinner plans due to illness.

What he notices: Interprets this as rejection. "She doesn't actually want to spend time with me. She's looking for excuses."

What he ignores: That this same friend called to reschedule and expressed genuine disappointment about canceling.

Client C — Core Belief: "I am worthless" (Worthlessness)

Situation: Team successfully completes a project she led.

What she notices: "The team did well. I didn't contribute much. Anyone could have managed this."

What she ignores: That she made key decisions, resolved conflicts, and was praised by her director for leadership.

Reflection: Why do clients "not see" contradicting evidence?

This is not willful ignorance. Core beliefs function as schemas — cognitive structures that organize information processing. Information consistent with the schema is processed fluently and quickly, while inconsistent information literally requires more cognitive effort to process. The brain takes the path of least resistance, confirming what it "already knows." This is why modification requires sustained, repeated, experiential work rather than a single logical argument.

Section 3

Intermediate Beliefs: Rules, Assumptions, and Attitudes

Intermediate beliefs sit between core beliefs and automatic thoughts. They serve as the bridge — translating deep-level beliefs into everyday operating rules that generate automatic thoughts in specific situations.

Three Types of Intermediate Beliefs

Type Format Examples
Rules "I must..." / "I should..." / "I should never..." "I must always be perfect." • "I should never show weakness."
Assumptions "If...then..." (conditional) "If I show vulnerability, people will reject me." • "If I work hard enough, people will accept me."
Attitudes Value judgments "It's terrible to make mistakes." • "Showing emotions is dangerous."

The Complete Chain: Core Belief to Automatic Thought

Understanding how these three levels connect is critical for case conceptualization. Here are two complete chains:

Client 1: Depression with Interpersonal Difficulties

Cognitive Chain — Client 1
Core Belief: "I am unlovable"
▼
Rule: "I must please everyone at all times"
▼
Assumption: "If I say no, people will abandon me"
▼
Situation: Friend asks her to help move on a day she's exhausted
▼
Automatic Thought: "If I say no, she'll hate me forever"

Client 2: Anxiety with Perfectionism

Cognitive Chain — Client 2
Core Belief: "I am incompetent"
▼
Rule: "I must never make a mistake"
▼
Assumption: "If I make a mistake, everyone will see I'm a fraud"
▼
Situation: About to send an email to colleagues
▼
Automatic Thought: "I need to proofread this 10 more times or I'll embarrass myself"
Test Yourself: Build a chain for a client with the core belief "I am worthless"

One possible chain:

Core Belief: "I am worthless"
Intermediate Rule: "I must always put others first"
Intermediate Assumption: "If I prioritize my own needs, I'm being selfish and people will confirm I don't deserve anything"
Situation: Colleague takes credit for her idea in a meeting
Automatic Thought: "It doesn't matter. My contribution wasn't that important anyway."

Notice how the core belief of worthlessness generates rules about self-sacrifice, which then filter the situation to produce a thought that minimizes the self.

Section 4

The Downward Arrow Technique

The Downward Arrow Technique is the primary method for uncovering core beliefs. It involves starting with an automatic thought and repeatedly asking "If that were true, what would it mean about you?" until you reach the deepest, most global self-statement.

Step-by-Step Protocol

Worked Example 1: Work-Related Depression

1
Start with the automatic thought
"My boss frowned when she read my report."
2
Therapist: "If that were true — if your boss was unhappy with your report — what would that mean to you?"
"It means my work isn't good enough."
3
Therapist: "And if your work isn't good enough, what does that mean about you?"
"It means I'm not competent at my job."
4
Therapist: "And if you're not competent at your job, what does that mean about you as a person?"
"I'm a failure." ← CORE BELIEF reached
How do you know you've reached a core belief? The statement becomes global ("I am..."), unconditional (no "if" or "when"), and the client often shows increased emotional intensity — sadness, tearfulness, or a sense of "hitting bottom."

Worked Example 2: Social Anxiety

1
Automatic thought
"People at the party were staring at me."
2
"If people were staring at you, what would that mean to you?"
"They were judging me. They could tell I was nervous."
3
"And if they were judging you for being nervous, what would that mean?"
"It means there's something wrong with me. Normal people don't get this anxious."
4
"And if there's something wrong with you, what does that mean about you as a person?"
"I'm defective. I'm fundamentally flawed." ← CORE BELIEF (Unlovability category)

Therapist Scripts

Key Phrases to Use
"If that were true, what would it mean to you?"
"And what does that say about you as a person?"
"What's the worst part about that for you?"
"What would that mean about you at the deepest level?"
"If [restate client's words], what does that mean about who you are?"

Practical Tips

  • When to use it: After you've identified a "hot" automatic thought that seems emotionally charged or appears repeatedly across situations.
  • How deep to go: Usually 3-5 questions deep. Stop when you reach a global "I am..." statement. Going further may lead to philosophical loops.
  • Handling emotional activation: If the client becomes very emotional, pause. Validate the emotion. Normalize. "It makes sense this feels painful — we've touched on something very deep." Don't push further until the client is regulated.
  • Timing in therapy: Generally not before session 3-4. The client needs trust, rapport, and some skill with automatic thoughts first.
  • Common pitfall: Clients sometimes give intermediate beliefs and stop ("I should never fail"). Gently continue: "And if you did fail, what would that mean about you?"
Practice: Trace this thought to a core belief: "My partner forgot our anniversary."

Possible downward arrow sequence:

AT: "My partner forgot our anniversary."
Q: "What does that mean to you?"
R: "It means I'm not important to him."
Q: "And if you're not important to him, what does that mean?"
R: "It means nobody truly cares about me."
Q: "And if nobody truly cares about you, what does that mean about you?"
R: "I'm unlovable." ← Core belief (Unlovability)

Alternative path could lead to worthlessness: "I don't matter enough to be remembered" → "I am insignificant" → "I am worthless."

Section 5

Modifying Core Beliefs

Why is this harder than changing automatic thoughts? Automatic thoughts are situation-specific and relatively "young." Core beliefs are deeply embedded schemas that have been reinforced for years or decades. They feel like identity, not thoughts. The client doesn't think "I believe I'm incompetent" — they simply experience themselves as incompetent. Modification requires persistent, multi-technique, experiential work over many sessions.

Five Core Techniques

1
Core Belief Log (Evidence For/Against)

The client systematically records evidence for and against the core belief across multiple situations and days. This is the cognitive equivalent of exposure — the client is forced to notice contradicting evidence they normally filter out.

Example: Client with "I am incompetent" logs each evening: "Evidence I was competent today" vs. "Evidence I was incompetent today." Over weeks, the ratio shifts.

2
Continuum Technique

Replaces all-or-nothing thinking with a 0-100% scale. Instead of "I am competent" vs. "I am incompetent," the client rates their competence on a continuum and places other people on it too.

Example: "Where would you put a surgeon who saved 100 lives? Where would you put someone who can't dress themselves? And where do you fall?" Clients almost never place themselves at 0 when forced to use a scale.

3
Historical Review of Evidence

Walk through the client's life chronologically and re-examine the evidence that formed the core belief. Often, childhood evidence was misinterpreted through a child's limited perspective.

Example: "Your mother criticized your schoolwork. As a child you concluded 'I'm stupid.' Looking at it now, what else might explain her behavior?" → "She was stressed and overwhelmed. She criticized everyone."

4
Developing a New, More Adaptive Core Belief

The goal is NOT to flip the belief 180 degrees ("I am perfectly competent") but to develop a nuanced, realistic alternative.

Example: Moving from "I am incompetent" to "I am competent in many areas and still learning in others." This is believable, flexible, and allows for normal imperfection.

5
Behavioral Experiments Targeting the Belief

Design real-world tests that directly challenge the belief. This is crucial because cognitive techniques alone rarely shift core beliefs — the client needs experiential evidence.

Example: Client with "I am unlovable" makes a vulnerable disclosure to a friend and observes the response. Prediction: "They will pull away." Actual outcome: "They thanked me for sharing and said they felt closer to me."

Test Yourself: Why is "I am totally competent" a poor replacement for "I am incompetent"?

Because it is just as rigid and absolute as the original belief. It will not hold up when the client inevitably makes a mistake or struggles with something, causing them to crash back to the original negative belief. Adaptive core beliefs must be flexible and realistic: "I am capable in many areas and can learn when I struggle" allows for normal human imperfection without threatening the client's self-concept.

Section 6

Common Mistakes in Working with Beliefs

Mistake 1: Targeting core beliefs too early.
Core belief work should not begin until the client has solid skills in identifying and challenging automatic thoughts (typically session 5+). Jumping to core beliefs prematurely can overwhelm the client and damage the therapeutic alliance. Build skills bottom-up: automatic thoughts first, then intermediate beliefs, then core beliefs.
Mistake 2: Making core beliefs too specific.
"My boss doesn't like me" is not a core belief — it's an automatic thought. Core beliefs are always global and unconditional: "I am unlovable," not "Person X doesn't love me." If the statement is about a specific situation or person, keep going deeper.
Mistake 3: Not distinguishing intermediate from core beliefs.
"I should always put others first" sounds deep, but it's an intermediate belief (a rule). The core belief underneath might be "I am worthless" or "I am only valuable if I serve others." Intermediate beliefs are conditional or prescriptive; core beliefs are unconditional declarations about the self.
Mistake 4: Challenging core beliefs with logic alone.
Telling a client "But look at all your accomplishments — clearly you're not incompetent!" will likely fail. The client will discount the evidence just as they always have. Core beliefs require experiential techniques (behavioral experiments, imagery rescripting, continuum work, emotion-focused methods) alongside cognitive restructuring. Head-level change must be paired with heart-level change.
Quick Identification Drill: Core, Intermediate, or Automatic?
  1. "She probably thinks I'm boring." → Automatic thought (situation-specific, about a particular person)
  2. "I must never burden others with my problems." → Intermediate belief (rule)
  3. "I am fundamentally flawed." → Core belief (global, unconditional, "I am...")
  4. "If I let my guard down, I'll get hurt." → Intermediate belief (assumption)
  5. "I am worthless." → Core belief
  6. "My presentation will go badly tomorrow." → Automatic thought
  7. "Showing emotions is weak." → Intermediate belief (attitude)
