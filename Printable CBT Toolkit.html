4. Cognitive Distortions Quick Reference

These are common thinking traps our minds fall into. Recognizing them is the first step to challenging unhelpful thoughts.

1. All-or-Nothing Thinking

Seeing things in black-and-white categories with no middle ground.
"If I'm not perfect, I'm a total failure."

2. Overgeneralization

Viewing a single negative event as a never-ending pattern.
"I got rejected — I'll always be alone."

3. Mental Filter

Dwelling on a single negative detail while ignoring positives.
Focusing on one critical comment despite ten compliments.

4. Disqualifying the Positive

Dismissing positive experiences as not counting.
"They only said that to be nice."

5. Jumping to Conclusions

Making negative interpretations without supporting evidence.
"She didn't text back — she must hate me."

6. Magnification / Minimization

Blowing things out of proportion or shrinking their importance.
Exaggerating a mistake; minimizing an achievement.

7. Emotional Reasoning

Assuming your feelings reflect reality.
"I feel stupid, so I must be stupid."

8. Should Statements

Rigid rules about how you or others "should" behave.
"I should be able to handle this without help."

9. Labeling

Attaching a fixed, global label instead of describing behavior.
"I'm a loser" instead of "I made a mistake."

10. Personalization

Blaming yourself for things outside your control.
"My child got a bad grade — I'm a terrible parent."

11. Catastrophizing

Expecting the worst-case scenario will definitely happen.
"This headache is probably a brain tumor."

12. Mind Reading

Assuming you know what others are thinking (negatively).
"Everyone in the room thinks I'm boring."

13. Fortune Telling

Predicting that things will turn out badly.
"I know the interview will go terribly."

14. Blaming

Holding others entirely responsible for your emotional pain.
"It's all your fault I feel this way."

15. Fallacy of Change

Expecting others will change to suit you if you pressure them.
"If he really loved me, he'd change for me."
